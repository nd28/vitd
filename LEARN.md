# Learning About Vitamin D

## The Basics

Vitamin D is a fat-soluble vitamin that your body produces when skin is exposed to UV-B radiation from sunlight. It exists in two forms:

- **D2 (ergocalciferol)** — found in some plants and mushrooms, less effective
- **D3 (cholecalciferol)** — produced by skin, found in animal foods, more effective at raising blood levels

**Always choose D3 supplements over D2.**

## What It Does

- Regulates calcium and phosphorus absorption (bone health)
- Supports immune function
- Affects mood and energy (receptors exist in the brain)
- Influences muscle function and recovery
- Plays a role in insulin sensitivity

## Why Indoor Workers Are At Risk

- Glass blocks UV-B completely — sitting near a window does nothing
- Most people get 90% of their vitamin D from sun, not food
- Office lighting produces zero UV
- Sunscreen SPF 30 reduces D production by ~97%
- Winter sun at latitudes above 35° has insufficient UV-B (the "vitamin D winter")

## Blood Test Numbers

| Range (ng/mL) | Status            |
|----------------|-------------------|
| 0–10           | Severe deficiency |
| 10–20          | Deficient         |
| 20–30          | Insufficient      |
| 30–50          | Low normal        |
| 50–60          | Normal            |
| 60–100         | Optimal           |
| >100           | Risk of toxicity  |

The test to ask for: **25-hydroxy vitamin D [25(OH)D]** — not 1,25(OH)2D.

## Absorption Rules

1. **Take with fat** — Vitamin D is fat-soluble. Taking it on an empty stomach wastes ~50% absorption. Take with your largest meal.
2. **Magnesium is required** — Magnesium activates vitamin D. Without enough magnesium, D stays inactive. Most deficient people are also low on magnesium.
3. **K2 partnership** — D increases calcium absorption; K2 directs that calcium into bones (not arteries). Take 100mcg K2 (MK-7) with your D3.
4. **Split doses** — 2000 IU twice daily may maintain steadier levels than 4000 IU once daily.

## Sun Exposure Guide

| Skin Type (Fitzpatrick) | Minutes needed (midday, arms+face exposed) |
|--------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| I–II (very fair)        | 10–15 min                                   |
| III–IV (medium)         | 15–25 min                                   |
| V–VI (dark)             | 25–40 min                                   |

- **Time of day:** 10am–2pm (when UV-B is present)
- **Expose skin:** Arms, face, hands — torso is even better if you can
- **No sunscreen** during your D-seeking minutes (apply after)
- **Clouds** only reduce UV-B by 20–30%
- **You cannot overdose from sun** — skin degrades excess D3 automatically

## Food Sources

| Food                    | Approx. IU per serving |
|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Salmon (wild)           | 600–1000 IU            |
| Salmon (farmed)         | 100–250 IU             |
| Sardines (canned)       | ~300 IU                |
| Mackerel                | ~350 IU                |
| Tuna (canned)           | ~200 IU                |
| Egg yolk                | ~40 IU                 |
| Fortified milk (1 cup)  | ~100 IU                |
| Fortified cereal        | ~40–100 IU             |
| UV-exposed mushrooms    | ~400–800 IU            |
| Cod liver oil (1 tsp)   | ~400–500 IU            |

**Tip:** Place mushrooms gill-side-up in direct sunlight for 15–60 minutes before cooking. They generate D2 from UV, similar to human skin.

## Deficiency Symptoms

Common symptoms of low vitamin D:

- Persistent fatigue
- Bone and lower back pain
- Muscle weakness / cramps
- Mood changes, low mood
- Frequent illness / slow recovery
- Hair loss
- Slow wound healing

**Many of these are non-specific.** The only way to know for sure is a blood test.

## Supplementation Guide

| Current Level  | Daily D3 Dose    | Re-test After |
|----------------|-------------------|---------------|
| <10 ng/mL      | 4000–6000 IU*     | 6–8 weeks     |
| 10–20 ng/mL    | 3000–4000 IU      | 6–8 weeks     |
| 20–30 ng/mL    | 2000–3000 IU      | 8–12 weeks    |
| 30–50 ng/mL    | 1000–2000 IU      | 3–6 months    |
| >50 ng/mL      | 800–1000 IU       | Annually       |

*Levels below 10 may require prescription loading doses (e.g., 60,000 IU/week for 8 weeks). See a doctor.

## Toxicity

Vitamin D toxicity (hypercalcemia) is rare but serious. It occurs only from supplements, never from sun.

**Signs of excess:**
- Nausea, vomiting
- Excessive thirst and urination
- Kidney stones
- Confusion

**Safe upper limit:** Most guidelines say 4000 IU/day is safe for adults. Toxicity typically occurs at >10,000 IU/day for months.

## Key Takeaways

1. If you work indoors, assume you're deficient until proven otherwise
2. Get tested — guessing is unreliable
3. D3 > D2, always with food, pair with K2 and magnesium
4. Sun is the best source — even 15 min at noon makes a difference
5. Re-test regularly — supplementation without testing is guessing
